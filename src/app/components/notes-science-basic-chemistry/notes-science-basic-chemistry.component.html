  1. Pure Substance: Homogeneous matter that cannot be separated into its components by physical means; with fixed composition and distinct properties
    1. Elements: Pure substance composed only of 1 type of atom; cannot be decomposed by ordinary means into simpler substances (Ex. H, He, Au, W)
    2. Compounds: Two or more elements chemically combined in a definite and constant proportion (Ex. KCl, CH3COOH, MgCl2)
      1. Ionic Compounds:
        1. Structural units are the cations and anions
        2. In the solid state, the ions do not move from their positions in the lattice but only vibrate in place
        3. Properties of Ionic Compounds:
          • Melting Point: High
          • Electrical Conductivity: Solid Non-conducting, Molten Conducting, Aqueous Conducting
          • Hardness: Very Hard
          • Malleability: Brittle
      2. Covalent Molecular Substances:
        • Uncharged or neutral structural units (molecules) in the crystal lattice.
        • The atoms in each molecule are held together by strong COVALENT BONDS.
        • Properties of Covalent Molecular Compounds:
          • Melting Point: Low
          • Electrical Conductivity: Solid Non-conducting, Molten Non-conducting, Aqueous Non-conducting
          • Hardness: Soft
          • Malleability: Brittle
      3. Covalent Network Substances:
        • The structural units that occupy the lattice points in the solid are ATOMS.
        • The atoms are bound to each other by strong COVALENT BONDS.
        • Properties of Covalent Network Substances:
          • Melting Point: Very high
          • Electrical Conductivity: Solid Non-conducting (except graphite), Molten Non-conducting, Aqueous Insoluble
          • Hardness: Very Hard
          • Malleability: Brittle
  2. Mixture: Combination of different substances in variable proportions; can be separated into its components by physical methods of separation
    1. Homogeneous: Uniform composition and properties throughout a given sample, but composition and properties may vary from one sample to another (e.g., solutions)
    2. Heterogeneous: With non-uniform properties throughout a sample where components retain their identity and phase boundaries exist (e.g., colloids, suspensions)

Properties of Matter

  1. Extensive Properties: Properties that depend on the amount of material observed
    • Examples:
      • Mass
      • Volume
      • Texture
  2. Intensive Properties: Properties that do not depend on the amount of material observed
    • Examples:
      • Density
      • Odor
      • Taste
  3. Extrinsic Properties: Properties that can vary with different samples of the same material
    • Examples:
      • Mass
      • Volume
      • Size
  4. Intrinsic Properties: Properties which are inherent to the substance and do not change for different samples of the same substance
    • Examples:
      • Density
      • Boiling and Melting Points
      • Odor
      • Taste
  5. Physical Properties: Characteristics observed or measured without changing the identity or composition of the material
  6. Chemical Properties: Characteristics observed or measured only by changing the identity or composition of the material; ability or inability of matter to undergo a change in its identity or composition at given conditions

Solubility Rules

  1. All nitrates are soluble.
  2. All acetates are soluble.
  3. All NH4+ salts are soluble.
  4. All salts of Group 1 are soluble.
  5. All chlorides are soluble except chlorides of Hg22+, Pb2+, and Ag+.
  6. All bromides are soluble except bromides of Hg22+, Pb2+, and Ag+.
  7. All iodides are soluble except iodides of Hg2+, Hg22+, Pb2+, and Ag+.
  8. Most sulfates are soluble except Group 2, Pb2+, and Hg22+.
  9. All phosphates are insoluble except NH4+ and Group 1.
  10. All chromates are insoluble except NH4+ and Group 1.

Neutralization: Reaction between an acid and a base forming water and salt

Laws of Chemical Combination

  1. Law of Conservation of Mass:

    States that the total mass of substances involved in a chemical reaction remains constant, regardless of the nature of the reaction or the physical state of the substances.

    Example: In a chemical reaction where hydrogen and oxygen combine to form water, the total mass of hydrogen and oxygen before the reaction is equal to the total mass of water after the reaction.

  2. Law of Definite Proportion or Composition - States that a given compound always contains exactly the same proportion of elements by mass, regardless of the source of the compound or how it was prepared.

    Example: Water (H2O) always consists of two hydrogen atoms for every one oxygen atom by mass, regardless of its source.

  3. Law of Multiple Proportion - States that when two elements form more than one compound, the ratios of the masses of one element that combine with a fixed mass of the other element are in simple whole numbers.

    Example: Carbon and oxygen can form two compounds, carbon monoxide (CO) and carbon dioxide (CO2). The ratio of masses of oxygen that combine with a fixed mass of carbon in these two compounds is in a simple whole number ratio.
