The Grammar Guide

Nouns

A noun names something- person, place, things, events, phenomena, emotions, etc.

  1. Proper Nouns - Names of specific persons, things, or places.

    Examples: John, Eiffel Tower, Google

  2. Common Nouns - Refer to any one of a class of persons, places, or things.

    Examples: dog, city, book

  3. Count Nouns - Nouns that can be counted and quantified using numbers.

    Examples: three apples, five cars, ten students

  4. Non-Count Nouns (Mass Nouns) - Nouns that cannot be counted and quantified using measurements.

    Examples: water, happiness, information

  5. Collective Nouns - Nouns that name a group of persons or things.

    Examples: team, flock, family

  6. Compound Nouns - Nouns that are made up of two words acting as a single unit.

    Examples: toothpaste, swimming pool, bookshelf

Verbs

  1. Action Verbs - These are verbs that express action. A majority of verbs are of this nature, and that is why most of the time, learners tend to define verbs as action words. Words like run, walk, talk, sing, dance, etc. are common examples of action verbs. We can further classify actions verbs into two more sub classifications-Regular and Irregular verbs.
    1. Regular verbs - Action verb is considered regular if its past form is derived by adding d or ed. Let us take the word walk for an example; the past form of walk is walked.
      Base Form Past Form
      Watch Watched
      Bake Baked
      Pick Picked
      Save Saved
    2. Irregular verbs - Action verb is considered irregular if its past form is derived not by adding d or ed, but through a change or through retention in spelling.
      Base Form Past Form
      Pay Paid
      Put Put
      Say Said
      Write Wrote
  2. Linking Verbs
    1. Linking Verbs as Copula - The copula is defined by Celce-Muricia as the link between the subject and non verbal predicates ( nouns, adjectives, and some adverbials). The copula also carries the tense and would determines subject-verb agreement.

      The weather is cold.

      In this example, "is" functions as a state copula, linking the subject "The weather" to the adjective "cold" to express its current condition or state.
    2. Linking Verbs as Perception Corpulas - Linking verbs, also known as perception copulas, connect the subject of a sentence to a subject complement that describes a sensory perception or condition.

      The flowers look beautiful.

      In this example, "look" links the subject "The flowers" to the adjective "beautiful," expressing a visual perception of their appearance.
    3. Linking Verb as State Copulas - State copulas are verbs that are not locomotive. They are more of a condition than an action.

      The book remains on the shelf.

      In this example, the linking verb "remains" connects the subject "The book" to the state or condition of being on the shelf. Unlike action verbs, which denote movement or activity, "remains" highlights a static state. It indicates that the book is in a state of rest or in a fixed position on the shelf, emphasizing the lack of action or movement.

  3. Helping Verbs - also known as auxiliary verbs, assist the main verb in a sentence by adding information about time, mood, or voice.

    She is reading a book.

    In this example, "is" is a helping verb that assists the main verb "reading" and indicates the present continuous tense.

    I have finished my homework.

    Here, "have" is a helping verb that, along with the main verb "finished," forms the present perfect tense, indicating a completed action with relevance to the present.
  4. Emphatic verbs - are verbs used to emphasize or intensify the action of the main verb in a sentence.

    She did finish her project.

    In this example, "did" is an emphatic verb that emphasizes the completion of the action, drawing attention to the fact that she indeed finished her project.

    I do love chocolate.

    Here, "do" serves as an emphatic verb, emphasizing the strong feeling of love for chocolate. It adds emphasis to the statement, making the sentiment more pronounced.
  5. Modals - helping verbs that convey a range of meanings, including expressing probability, attitudes, and performing social functions. They are paired with a base verb. Examples:
    1. Stating Ability

      I can do anything.

      The modal "can" indicates the ability to perform a task.
    2. Expressing Regret

      I should have loved you.

      The modal "should" expresses regret for not having loved in the past.
    3. Giving Warning

      You may be in danger.

      The modal "may" suggests a possibility and warns about potential danger.
    4. Expressing Admission with Reservation

      I might be wrong, but I know what I did.

      The modal "might" admits a possibility of being wrong while asserting awareness of one's actions.
    5. Expressing Observation

      You must do this.

      The modal "must" emphasizes the necessity of performing a task based on observation or requirement.

Adverbs

Adverbs modify verbs, adjectives, or another adverb.

  1. Adverb of Manner - answers the question how a thing is done

    She completed the puzzle quickly.

    Question: How did she completed the puzzle?
    Answer: quickly
  2. Adverb of Time - answers the question when the action happened

    They will arrive here tomorrow.

    Question: When will they arrive?
    Answer: tomorrow
  3. Adverb of Frequency - This answers the question how often

    I visit my grandparents often.

    Question: How frequent will you visit your grandparents
    Answer: often

Prepositions

Prepositions are words that show the relationship between a noun (or pronoun) and other elements in a sentence, indicating location, time, direction, or relationships.

The cat is under the table.

In this example, "under" is a preposition that shows the spatial relationship between the cat and the table.

We'll meet at the park at noon.

Here, "at" is used to indicate both the location (park) and the time (noon), showcasing the versatility of prepositions in expressing various relationships.

Conjunctions

Conjunctions serve to link words or groups of words, creating a cohesive unit within a sentence.

  1. Coordinating Conjunctions - Coordinating conjunctions connect words of equal grammatical importance, coordinating their roles in a sentence.

    She likes coffee and tea.

    He works hard but enjoys his free time.

  2. Correlative Conjunctions - Correlative conjunctions always appear in pairs, connecting words with similar grammatical structures and maintaining balance in the sentence.

    You can have either pizza or pasta.

    Neither the cat nor the dog is at home.

  3. Subordinating Conjunctions - Subordinating conjunctions link an independent clause with a dependent clause, establishing a relationship where one idea is dependent on the other.

    She will go out whenever it stops raining.

    We'll eat outside unless it's too cold.

Interjections

Interjections are words that convey strong emotions or reactions, often standing alone as independent sentences with the speaker as the implicit subject.

Ouch! That hurt.

In this example, "Ouch" expresses pain, standing independently as a complete sentence.

Aha! I finally understood the puzzle.

Here, "Aha" conveys a moment of realization, functioning as a standalone sentence indicating the speaker's excitement.

Interjections add emotional tone to language, reflecting the speaker's immediate reactions or sentiments. They are distinct in that they often don't play a grammatical role within a sentence structure but rather convey the speaker's feelings.

Subject - Verb Agreement

  1. Collective Nouns: These can take a singular or plural verb based on whether the group is perceived as a single entity or individuals.

    Our debate team has won the competition.

    The debate team is treated as a singular unit achieving a collective victory.
  2. Nouns Ending in -s and -ics: Always paired with singular verbs.

    Mathematics is my favorite subject.

    The singular verb "is" aligns with the non-count nature of "Mathematics."
  3. Nouns in Sets of Twos: Paired with a singular verb if "pair" is present; otherwise, a plural verb.

    A pair of scissors is on the table.

    The presence of "pair" signals singularity in this context.
  4. "A Number" and "The Number": "A number" takes a plural verb; "The number" takes a singular verb.

    A number of students have been expelled.

    The number of students being expelled is rising.

    "A number" implies multiple students, while "the number" indicates a singular count.
  5. Fraction and Percent: Singular verb if paired with a non-count noun, and vice versa.

    50% of the Earth’s water is still safe for drinking.

    The singular verb aligns with the non-countable nature of "water."
  6. Compound Nouns: Joined by a conjunction, compound subjects take plural verbs.

    Paul and Robin are brothers.

    The plural verb aligns with the plural subject "Paul and Robin."
  7. Neither...nor and Either...or: Verb agrees with the closest noun.

    Neither John nor his brothers are going to America.

    The verb agrees with the plural noun "brothers."
  8. As Well and Together With: Verb agrees with the first noun (subject).

    The president, together with his advisers, is leaving tomorrow.

    The singular verb aligns with the singular subject "president."

Tenses

  1. Simple Present Tense: Verbs express habitual or factual actions in the present.

    She sings.

    This tense is used to express actions that are habitual, routine, or facts in the present. In this case, it highlights the habitual action of singing.
  2. Simple Past Tense: Indicates actions completed in the past.

    She sang yesterday.

    Signifying actions completed in the past, this tense is employed to narrate events that occurred at a specific time, such as yesterday in this instance.
  3. Simple Future Tense: Expresses actions to occur in the future.

    She will sing.

    Future actions are conveyed with this tense. Here, it indicates the intention or likelihood of singing in the future.
  4. Present Progressive Tense: Describes actions happening at the moment.

    She is singing.

    Describes actions happening at the moment of speaking. It indicates the ongoing activity of singing in the present.
  5. Past Progressive Tense: Expresses a continuing action that started and ended in the past.

    She was singing all afternoon yesterday.

    Expresses a continuing action that was happening in the past over a specified duration, like the entire afternoon.
  6. Future Progressive Tense: Describes a continuing action in the future.

    She will be singing in the competition.

    Describes a continuing action that will be happening in the future, specifically during a competition.
  7. Present Perfect Tense: Expresses an action that started in the past and continues into the present.

    I have sung a song.

    Indicates an action that started in the past and continues into the present. Here, the singing occurred at some indefinite time before now.
  8. Past Perfect Tense: Expresses two past actions, with one happening before the other.

    She had sung before she danced.

    Expresses two past actions, with one completed before the other. In this case, singing occurred before dancing.
  9. Future Perfect Tense: Indicates two future actions, with one occurring before the other.

    She will have sung before she dances.

    Indicates two future actions, with one expected to be completed before the other. Singing precedes dancing in this context.
  10. Present Perfect Progressive Tense: Describes an action that started in the past, continues in the present, and likely extends into the future.

    She has been singing since this morning.

    Describes an ongoing action that started in the past, continues in the present, and likely extends into the future. Here, it emphasizes continuous singing since the morning.
  11. Past Perfect Progressive Tense: Expresses two past actions, with the first one ongoing when the second one transpired.

    She had been singing before the concert started.

    Expresses two past actions, with the first one ongoing when the second one transpired. The singing was in progress when the concert started.
  12. Future Perfect Progressive Tense: Describes two future actions, with the first action ongoing when the second one transpires.

    She will have been singing before the concert started.

    Describes two future actions, with the first ongoing when the second one transpires. In this case, singing is expected to be ongoing when the concert started.

Four Kinds of Sentences according to Function

  1. Declarative: Makes a statement or expresses an opinion.

    She sings beautifully.

    This example asserts that "she sings beautifully."
  2. Interrogative: Asks a question.

    Does she sing beautifully?

    This example inquires about the quality of her singing.
  3. Imperative: Gives a command or makes a request.

    Sing beautifully.

    This imperative instructs someone to sing beautifully.
  4. Exclamatory: Conveys strong emotion or surprise.

    How beautifully she sings!

    This exclamation expresses admiration for her singing.

Four Kinds of Sentences According to Structure

  1. S-AV-DO (Subject-Action Verb-Direct Object): Describes an action performed by the subject on the direct object.

    She eats apples.

    Action (eating) performed by the subject (she) on the direct object (apples).
  2. S-AV-IO-DO (Subject-Action Verb-Indirect Object-Direct Object): Illustrates an action where the subject gives the direct object to the indirect object.

    He gives her flowers.

    Action (giving) where the subject (he) gives the direct object (flowers) to the indirect object (her).
  3. S-AV-DO-OC (Subject-Action Verb-Direct Object-Object Complement): Involves an action where the subject transforms the direct object into an object complement.

    They painted the wall red.

    Action (painted) where the subject (they) transforms the direct object (wall) into an object complement (red).
  4. S-LV-PN (Subject-Linking Verb-Predicate Nominative): Uses a linking verb to connect the subject to the predicate nominative.

    She is a doctor.

    Linking verb (is) connects the subject (she) to the predicate nominative (doctor).
  5. S-LV-PA (Subject-Linking Verb-Predicate Adjective): Employs a linking verb to link the subject to the predicate adjective.

    The flowers smell fragrant.

    Linking verb (smell) links the subject (flowers) to the predicate adjective (fragrant).

Four Kinds of Sentences According to Structure

  1. Simple Sentence: Consists of one independent clause, conveying a complete thought or idea.

    She sings.

    Example of a simple sentence with one independent clause.
  2. Compound Sentence: Comprises two independent clauses joined by a coordinating conjunction or a semicolon.

    She sings, and he dances.

    Example of a compound sentence with two independent clauses.
  3. Complex Sentence: Contains one independent clause and at least one dependent clause connected by a subordinating conjunction.

    While she sings, he dances.

    Example of a complex sentence with one independent and one dependent clause.
  4. Compound-Complex Sentence: Involves a combination of two or more independent clauses and at least one dependent clause.

    Although she sings, he dances, and they both enjoy it.

    Example of a compound-complex sentence with multiple independent and dependent clauses.
